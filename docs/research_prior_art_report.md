# Sector Specific NLP Alpha Generation and LLM Trading Systems: A Research Review

## Executive Summary

Using natural language processing and large language models to extract alpha from unstructured text is a rapidly growing field in quantitative finance [1]. This review examines academic literature and industry frameworks to understand how models can generate predictive trading signals for specific stock sectors. 

Our findings indicate that the most significant opportunities for large language models to generate alpha lie in highly technical, low-readability news sectors where information diffusion is slow and retail investors struggle to interpret developments [2] [3]. The quantum computing sector represents a prime example of such an environment. This review analyzes key methodologies, empirical results, architectural frameworks, and critical limitations of prior work to guide the design of the Quantum Alpha Intelligence Platform.

## Key Academic and Industry Prior Art

### Lopez-Lira and Tang (2024): Return Predictability and Large Language Models

This seminal study from the University of Florida investigates the capability of large language models to predict stock market returns using news headlines [2]. 

The researchers documented that advanced models like GPT-4 can accurately predict stock price movements from news, outperforming traditional sentiment analysis methods [2]. A long-short strategy that bought stocks with positive recommendations and sold those with negative recommendations earned a cumulative return of over 400% from October 2021 to December 2022, achieving a Sharpe ratio of 3.8 with GPT-4 [2]. 

Crucially, the study found that this return predictability is significantly more pronounced among smaller stocks and following negative news [2]. Furthermore, only sophisticated models passing a certain complexity threshold were able to extract helpful trading signals from low-readability, complex news, while basic models like BERT failed [2]. This suggests that technical translation and return forecasting are emergent capabilities of larger language models [2].

### GPT-Signal (Wang et al., 2024): Automated Feature Engineering in Alpha Research

This study from the University of Toronto presents a framework for using generative artificial intelligence to automate the feature engineering process in quantitative trading [4]. 

Rather than relying solely on human intuition, the researchers leveraged GPT-4 to interpret historical signal data and generate novel, return-predictive formulaic alphas [4]. The framework was tested across three S&P 500 sectors: Information Technology, Health Care, and Energy [4]. 

The empirical results showed that the GPT-generated signals consistently outperformed baseline signals across all selected sectors over a five-year period from 2016 to 2020 [4]. The model demonstrated a unique capability to combine existing indicators in non-linear, higher-order ways that human researchers typically overlook, validating the use of sophisticated models for sector-specific alpha mining [4].

### TradExpert (Ding et al., 2025): Mixture of Expert LLMs for Trading

This paper from the Université de Montréal and Mila introduces a novel framework that employs a Mixture of Experts approach to quantitative trading [5]. 

The system utilizes four specialized models, each dedicated to analyzing a distinct source of financial data: news texts, historical market data, quantitative alpha factors, and company fundamental data [5]. The individual reports generated by these specialized models are then synthesized by a General Expert model to make a final trading decision [5]. 

The study demonstrated that this structured division of labor, mimicking real-world analyst teams, consistently outperformed single-model baselines across all trading scenarios, highlighting the value of multi-source data integration [5].

### Sector-Level Sentiment Analysis (Almalis et al., 2022)

This study proposes a deep learning solution trained exclusively on financial news to perform sector-level sentiment analysis [6]. 

The researchers combined multiple recurrent neural networks with a semi-supervised learning method to detect and correct mislabeled data [6]. They proposed an ensemble of sentiment and sector-classification models to predict which specific economic sectors would be affected by incoming news articles [6]. 

The study demonstrated that sector-specific sentiment analysis compared favorably against traditional state-of-the-art models and general market-wide sentiment analysis, confirming that focusing on sector-specific dynamics yields more accurate predictive signals [6].

### Industry Sentiment and Information Diffusion (Truong, 2025)

This paper examines whether industry-specific sentiment explains industry-level stock returns [3]. 

The researcher documented that while market-wide sentiment captures broad, systematic mispricing, industry-specific sentiment reveals unique, sector-level opportunities [3]. This is primarily due to gradual information diffusion within specific sectors, where technical or specialized information takes longer to be fully digested and priced in by the broader market [3]. This slow diffusion creates exploitable opportunities for investors who can interpret specialized industry data early [3].

### End-to-End LLM Enhanced Trading System (Zhou and Mehra, 2025)

This project from Columbia University introduces an end-to-end trading system that leverages FinGPT for real-time market sentiment analysis [7]. 

The system ingests live data from financial news and social media, processes and summarizes the text, and utilizes FinGPT to generate granular sentiment scores [7]. These sentiment signals are then integrated with traditional technical indicators (such as simple moving averages, relative strength index, and stochastic oscillators) to generate actionable buy and sell signals, which are displayed on a real-time dashboard [7]. This architecture validates the practical, real-time integration of financial language models into interactive trading systems [7].

## Methodological Frameworks

The prior art reveals several successful methodological patterns for building NLP-driven trading systems.

### Input Data Modalities

Successful systems combine unstructured textual data with structured quantitative metrics. Unstructured inputs include financial news headlines, company press releases, SEC regulatory filings, and academic paper preprints [2] [5] [7]. Structured inputs include historical price and volume data, quantitative alpha factors, and fundamental corporate metrics [5] [7].

### Model Specialization and Fine-Tuning

Domain-specific models consistently outperform general-purpose models on financial tasks [10]. Fine-tuning approaches like FinBERT and FinGPT, which are pre-trained on vast financial corpora, demonstrate superior contextual comprehension and numerical sensitivity compared to general base models [7] [10]. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods like LoRA and QLoRA are the industry standard for adapting lightweight models to these specialized domains [10].

### Signal Extraction and Structuring

To convert raw text into actionable signals, sophisticated systems prompt or fine-tune models to output structured data formats (typically JSON) containing:
- Sentiment classification (strongly bearish to strongly bullish) [2] [7]
- Event classification (mapping text to specific sector catalysts) [5] [6]
- Confidence scores and logits [7]
- Target entity and ticker tagging [5]
- Contextual explanations or "technical translations" [2] [4]

## Critical Limitations and Lessons for Our Project

While the academic literature confirms the predictive power of large language models, a recent comprehensive study by Li et al. (2026) offers critical cautionary insights [11].

Systematic backtests over a longer-term evaluation and a broader cross-section of stocks revealed that previously reported large language model advantages can deteriorate significantly [11]. The researchers found that strategies relying solely on model-generated sentiment tend to be overly conservative in bull markets (underperforming passive benchmarks) and overly aggressive in bear markets (incurring heavy losses) [11]. 

This suggests that large language models struggle to adapt to changing market regimes and macroeconomic shifts when used as automated traders [11].

### Design Principles for the Quantum Alpha Intelligence Platform

Based on these findings and limitations, we will design our platform around three core principles:

1. **Focus on Technical Translation, Not Return Prediction**: Instead of positioning the platform as an automated stock predictor (which is prone to market regime failure), we will frame it as a **Technical Intelligence Translator**. The primary value lies in converting complex quantum physics jargon (such as logical qubit thresholds, gate fidelities, and error correction overhead) into clear explanations of commercial relevance. This helps human investors make better-informed decisions, leveraging the model's strengths in language processing while leaving macroeconomic judgment to the human user.

2. **Isolate Niche Sector Inefficiency**: As documented by Truong (2025), industry-specific sentiment has strong predictive power due to gradual information diffusion [3]. Quantum computing is a highly specialized, niche sector where information diffuses exceptionally slowly because very few market participants understand the physics. By focusing exclusively on this sector, we maximize the model's informational advantage.

3. **Multi-Source Ingestion**: Following the TradExpert (2025) methodology, we will ingest not just news, but also academic preprints (arXiv) and regulatory disclosures (SEC EDGAR) [5]. This allows the platform to capture technical milestones at the source (arXiv) before they are translated into corporate press releases or financial news, capturing the earliest possible signal in the information diffusion chain.
